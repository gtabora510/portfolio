About the Developer Portal Content Style Guide

The Developer Portal content style guide outlines the guidelines to be followed to establish and protect the written style and to ensure consistency in its use.

This content is continuously iterated to ensure it supports the developer portal communications strategy, priorities, and deliverables.

This guidance covers how developer portal content should be written (portal user interface content, notices, procedures, documentation, and so on.)

Guidance is provided on recommended usage as well as usage that should be avoided.

The writing guidance provided in this document is for internal communications, products, and events only. If your team has public-facing events that require official communications, follow the external communications guidelines.

Content Suggestions/Questions

If you have suggestions or questions about the contents of this document, send them to developer.portal@acme.com.

Developer Portal Persona

The Developer Portal is for the company’s engineers. Therefore, the foundational principle of the portal is to create content from an engineer’s perspective.

  • Think about how an engineer would typically interact with the portal and your product, including onboarding or provisioning for the first time.
  • Consider how an engineer might use your documentation or the information you are providing to solve specific issues or achieve certain objectives.

Voice and Tone

Developer portal content should be written in a succinctly conversational tone and voice. This means that developer portal content should be informative and direct without being overly conversational. Engineers are accessing this content to find out how to do something. You want them to find what they need and be able to implement what they learn without extraneous words.

A succinctly conversational tone means that you should avoid being too friendly in your writing; do not use words, such as it’s, easy, simply, or let’s. Conversely, try not to be stilted or overly dry in your writing. Write your content in short, concise sentences. Aim to keep your sentences to less than 25 words, if possible.

Imagine an engineer is sitting next to you, and you are clearly and directly guiding them through the steps to complete a task. The following examples illustrate differences in tone.

Avoid Recommended
Oops! Something’s wrong with the update process. Let’s see what went wrong and how you can fix it.

This is the correct tone.

Timeless Content

Timeless content refers to documentation that does not contain references to time-based words, such as currently, soon, now, new, at this time, latest, and so on.

Avoiding these words in documentation is important to ensuring its relevance and reducing the effort required to maintain it.

Time-based words may be used when writing time-specific content, such as release notes or incident communications.

Punctuation

Use a serial or Oxford comma when listing three or more items in a sentence. While the serial comma is not preferred in the ACME style guide, it is commonly used in technical writing to aid in reader understanding.

Example: Susan was invited to a meeting with a new hire, her former manager and an HR professional. In this example, Susan is meeting with a new hire who happens to be her former manager and works as an HR professional. When you add the serial comma, the meaning changes to indicate that Susan is meeting with three people: Susan was invited to a meeting with a new hire, her former manager, and an HR professional.

Be mindful of where periods are placed when ending your sentence with a parenthesis. As a general rule, the period should fall inside the parentheses if the entire sentence is inside the parentheses. In contrast, the period should fall outside the parentheses if only a part of the sentence is inside the parentheses.

In a bulleted list, whether you add a period to the items in a bulleted list depends on if any of the items in the list are a complete sentence.

  • If none of the items in a bulleted list is a complete sentence, you do not need to add a period at the end.
  • If any one of the items in a bulleted list is a complete sentence, you should add a period to all the items in the list.

Release Notes

Release notes are a summary of what has changed with your product since the last release. They are important when highlighting a change that will directly affect your user’s experience with your product. Whether it is a new feature within your product, a fix to something that was previously broken, or a change to the way it looks, release notes act as a guide for your user.

The following are the benefits of release notes:

  • They show progress: Frequent and consistent product updates give users confidence that your product is evolving and delivering to its roadmap.
  • They foster engagement: Release notes are a great way to engage your users at regular intervals by showing them what is new in your product.
  • They educate users: You want your users to get as much value out of your product as they can, and release notes can be a great way to educate them on how best to use any new updates.
  • They set user expectations: By informing and educating users on why a change in the product is better for them, you can head off some change resistance.

You should write a release note for the following:

  • New features: It is important to update your users on what is new and how it benefits the user.
  • Bug fixes: If there was a specific problem in your product and you corrected it, be sure to let your users know.
  • Product improvements: Inform your users of enhancements to the product. Users want to know that the product is evolving.
  • Product deprecations: Inform your users when features are removed. This shows that the team is mindful of removing unused or outdated features.

Release note subject line guidance:

Follow this structure to help provide consistency in product release notes.

[Product Name] [announces or another verb] [feature / event summary]

Examples:

  • Developer portal announces the release of in-context help
  • Estate Management releases Security Toolkit 11

Announcements

An announcement may be used to provide product or product line information that falls outside of a release note. This could include information on product feature launch dates, upcoming events, or conferences where your product will be highlighted, updates to product documentation, or information regarding languages that are becoming end of life or platforms that are being retired.

When writing the announcement, be mindful of your objective. What is that you want the reader to know or do after reading the announcement? If due dates are involved, be sure that they clearly called out and that they are accurate.

Announcement subject line guidance:

Follow this structure to help provide consistency in product announcements.

[Product Name] [announces or another verb] [event summary]

Examples:

  • Developer portal invites you to attend an onboarding session
  • OpenRewrite announces upcoming launch of 40 recipes

Incident Notifications

Incident notifications inform users of an issue with a product that is impacting users, but is not causing a service interruption.

Incident notifications should have a minimum of three messages associated with them.

  • Incident notice â€” message issued as soon as possible after the issue has been detected.
  • Incident update â€” message issued to communicate that the issue is ongoing and provide an overview of the resolution and an estimated resolution time if possible.
  • Incident resolved â€” message issued when the issue is resolved and service is returned to normal.

Incident subject line guidance:

Follow this structure to help provide consistency in service issue notifications.

Incident notice with [Product Name] [optional brief descriptor of issue] Incident update with [Product Name] [optional brief descriptor of issue] Incident resolved with [Product Name] [optional brief descriptor of issue]

Examples:

  • Incident notice with GAIA Portal experiencing latency
  • Incident update with GAIA Portal experiencing latency
  • Incident resolved with GAIA Portal experiencing latency

Planned Outage Notifications

Planned outage notifications inform users of the timing and duration of a planned service interruption. Any actions that the user should take prior to the outage window or after the outage window should also be conveyed.

Planned outage notifications should have a minimum of three messages associated with them: advanced notice, outage initiated, and service restored.

  • Advanced notice â€” message issued far enough ahead of the planned outage window to allow users to plan for the service interruption. Two weeks in advance is a suggested minimum for this type of notification.
  • Outage initiated â€” message issued when the planned outage window has started.
  • Service restored â€” message issued when the planned outage window has closed and service has successfully been restored.
  • Outage issue â€” message issued when issues are encountered during the planned outage which result in a delay to service restoration.

Unplanned Outage Notifications

User Action Required

User action notifications communicate the need for your product’s users to take an action and the date by which it should be taken.

User action notifications may be used for a variety of reasons but the underlying use is a call to action with a due date.

You should ensure that the content of the user action notification clearly communicates:

  • The action to be taken
  • Directions for completing the action
  • The due date for the action
  • Where users can access help if needed.

User action notifications should have a minimum of two messages â€“ notice of the action and a reminder to complete the action.

  • Notice of action â€” message to create awareness of the action required, its impact to you, its due date, and a means for you to ask questions or learn more.
  • Reminder to complete â€” message to remind users of action required and due date.

User action required subject line guidance:

Follow this structure to help provide consistency in user action notifications.

Action Required for [Product Name] users by [Date] Reminder: Action Required for [Product Name] users by [Date]

Examples:

  • Action Required for Developer Portal users by December 15, 2024
  • Reminder: Action Required for Developer Portal users by December 15, 2024
